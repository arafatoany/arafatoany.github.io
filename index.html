About Me

I am a doctoral researcher in Biomedical Sciences at Texas A&M University, where my work focuses on uncovering novel therapeutic strategies for aggressive and drug-resistant cancers. My research centers on ferroptosis, an iron-dependent form of regulated cell death, and its potential to selectively target cancer cells that evade conventional treatments.

In the Molecular & Cellular Oncology Laboratory, I investigate the mechanistic role of nuclear receptor signaling pathways—particularly the NR4A1/NR4A2–SP1–CD71 axis—in regulating iron metabolism, oxidative stress, and ferroptotic sensitivity. My work integrates molecular biology, redox biology, and translational pharmacology to develop innovative cancer therapeutics.

Prior to my PhD, I completed my graduate studies in Biotechnology and Genetic Engineering, where I conducted research on bacteriophages targeting multidrug-resistant pathogens and explored protein function prediction using computational approaches. These experiences shaped my interdisciplinary perspective, bridging microbiology, computational biology, and cancer research.

I am deeply committed to advancing translational science, mentoring emerging researchers, and contributing to collaborative, high-impact research environments. My long-term goal is to lead an independent research program focused on developing precision therapeutics for complex diseases.
